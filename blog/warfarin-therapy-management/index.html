Warfarin Therapy Management

By Golnar Mahani August 07, 2017

In this post the research work of B. Yet et al. titled, “Decision support system for Warfarin therapy management using Bayesian networks” has been reviewed.

Warfarin is an anticoagulant which is used for embolism and thrombosis prevention. The influence of this anticoagulant is examined by a normalized ratio of blood coagulation time of patient to a reference sample, known as International Normalized Ratio (INR). Since the response of patients to warfarin is different, the process of warfarin therapy is very challenging. Such variations in responses are caused by factors known as variation factors, including drug effects and comorbidities. Accordingly, assisting physicians with managing warfarin therapy through a decision support system is essential.
In this paper, four major questions are covered in developing the warfarin therapy DSS:

  1. How much dose should be adjusted for a specific patient?
  2. When should the patient take the next INR test?
  3. What are the cerebral bleeding and stroke risks for the patient?
  4. What are the variation factors that affect the response to the therapy?

The following figure shows previous proposed DSS for Warfarin therapy. In the proportional-derivative and Bayes forecasting models the variation factors are not taken into account. Regression models do not provide dose adjustments for long-term therapies. Moreover, regression models and rule-based models require all the specified inputs to provide the user with an output. One of the other limitations of rule-based models is that the models could not provide a support for finding the cause of an unexpected INR increase.

Taking into account the limitations and drawbacks of the previous models, B. Yet et al. proposed a Bayesian network model for Warfarin therapy management.

Model Construction

The patients were selected from those who received Warfarin therapy for more than 14 days (maintenance phase), because of the large number of patients and frequent patient status monitoring in this phase.
The variation factors and the relation parameters between these factors and INR are elicited from experts. Since the conditional probability table (CPT) of INR in the proposed Bayesian model grows exponentially, parent divorcing technique and Noisy Max model were used to reduce the number of probabilities needed to be elicited. The remaining CPTs are learned from data. For these CPTs the data were preprocessed and after that the expectation maximization algorithm was used.
For the probability elicitation by experts, two methods were used. In the first method, one expert was asked to express the conditional probabilities using numerical or verbal anchors. In the second method, the expert was asked to imagine 1000 Warfarin therapies to express the probability of a specified outcome of the specific parameter among those therapies. To reduce the errors of these methods, the authors suggested providing the expert the sensitivity analysis plots of INR, Dose change and follow-up intervals to the elicited variables.

The evaluation results show that the model has acceptable performance in dose recommendations, follow-up interval decisions and enhancement of variation factor investigation. However, the model could be more reliable if the authors have used more than one expert in the process of probability elicitation. Moreover, the models for bleeding and stroke risks should have been developed based on different patients not just the data of patients with atrial fibrillation. Also, in order to have a more generalized model, the initiation phase of Warfarin therapy should have been considered, since this phase is a critical dosing period with large amount of uncertainty and risks for adverse events.

References

B. Yet, K. Bastani, H. Raharjo, S. Lifvergren, W. Marsh, B. Bergman, “Decision support system for Warfarin therapy management using Bayesian networks,” Decis. Support Syst., vol. 55, no. 2, pp. 488–498, May 2013.

C. R. Lee, “Warfarin Initiation and the Potential Role of Genomic-Guided Dosing,” Clin. Med. Res., Vol. 3, no. 4, pp. 205-206, Nov. 2005.
